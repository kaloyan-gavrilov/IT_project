H I S T O R Y

The Porsche 911 Turbo, internally known as the 930, was developed in the 1970s to comply with FIA homologation regulations for Group 4 competition. Porsche needed to produce 400 cars within a twenty-four-month period to gain approval for Group 4, and the 911 Turbo quickly became popular among car enthusiasts. The car utilized a single KKK turbocharger and had a total power output of 260 PS at 5,500 rpm and 329 Nm of torque at 4,000 rpm. The platform also had a revised suspension, larger brakes, a stronger gearbox, and a "whale tail" rear spoiler for increased downforce.


However, the car was demanding to drive, prone to oversteer and turbo-lag, and was nicknamed "the Widowmaker" due to several crashes and deaths attributed to its handling characteristics. Porsche made changes to the 930 for the 1978 model year, including enlarging the engine bore, adding an air-to-air intercooler, and upgrading the brakes to units similar to those used on the 917 race car. While the changes increased power output and torque, they also increased the weight of the vehicle, contributing to a substantial change in the handling and character of the car compared to earlier models. The 930 was eventually withdrawn from markets in Japan and the United States due to changing emissions regulations.
